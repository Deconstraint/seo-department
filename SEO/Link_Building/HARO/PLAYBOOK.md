# HARO / Source Request Playbook

> **Tactic:** HARO (Help a Reporter Out) & Source Request Platforms
> **Difficulty:** Low–Medium | **Scalability:** Medium | **Link Quality:** Very High
> **Time to First Link:** 1–3 weeks per placement

---

## 1. When to Use This Tactic

Use HARO and Source Requests when:
- You want high-authority editorial links from national news, major magazines, and top-tier publications (DR 70–90+)
- The client has a named spokesperson or subject matter expert (SME) who can be quoted
- You need links from publications that don't accept guest posts or paid placements
- You want to build brand authority and credibility alongside the link
- The client is an expert in an area where journalists frequently seek comment (finance, health, tech, real estate, law, marketing, business)

**Why it works:** Journalists actively need expert sources for their stories. By positioning the client as the go-to expert, you earn editorial links from major publications without cold pitching — the journalist comes to you with an open slot.

**Platform Ecosystem (Post-HARO Acquisition):**
- **Connectively (formerly HARO)** — connectively.us — primary platform, now subscription-based
- **Qwoted** — qwoted.com — used by major business/finance journalists
- **SourceBottle** — sourcebottle.com — strong for B2B and SMB queries
- **ProfNet (PR Newswire)** — profnet.prnewswire.com — high-end publications
- **Twitter/X #journorequest** — real-time reactive opportunities
- **Help a B2B Writer** — helpab2bwriter.com — specifically B2B content writers
- **Featured.com** — featured.com — expert quote platform

**Do NOT use HARO when:**
- The client has no willing spokesperson or SME who can be quoted by name
- The client is in a highly regulated field with strict communications policies (some financial/legal firms prohibit employee media quotes)
- You need link volume quickly — HARO is a "quality over quantity" tactic
- The client's domain has no brand presence (journalists Google the expert before quoting them)

## 2. Prospecting Criteria & Qualification Framework

### Query Qualification Checklist
Before responding to a HARO query, evaluate:
- [ ] **Topical relevance:** Does the query directly match the client's expertise? (Stretch matches = low acceptance)
- [ ] **Deadline:** Is there enough time to craft a quality response? (Average HARO deadline: 24–72 hours)
- [ ] **Publication quality:** Is the journalist from a DR 50+ publication? (Check their outlet)
- [ ] **Journalist legitimacy:** Does the journalist have a real byline? Is the outlet listed on the query?
- [ ] **Query specificity:** Is the journalist asking for something specific (data, experience, expert opinion)? Or is it too vague?
- [ ] **Spokesperson match:** Does the client's expert have genuine knowledge and credentials in this exact area?
- [ ] **Exclusivity:** Is this a scoop opportunity where being first matters, or a general expert call where you have time to craft quality?

### Scoring Queries (0–3 points per category)
| Category | 0 | 1 | 2 | 3 |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Publication DR | < 30 | 30–50 | 50–70 | 70+ |
| Topic Relevance | Tangential | Related | Direct match | Core expertise |
| Expert Credentials | No match | Partial | Strong | Perfect match |
| Deadline | < 2 hours | 2–12 hours | 12–48 hours | 48+ hours |

**Respond to any query scoring 7+. Skip anything scoring 5 or below.**

### Expert Spokesperson Requirements
To maximize acceptance rates, the client's expert should have:
- Real name and professional title that matches the query topic
- Verifiable credentials (LinkedIn profile, company website bio, publications)
- Ability to provide original insights, data, or specific experience (not just generic opinions)
- Willingness to be quoted publicly by name
- Response availability within 2–4 hours of query receipt (competitive queries)

## 3. Step-by-Step Process

### Phase 1: Setup & Infrastructure (Days 1–3)

**Step 1: Set Up HARO and Platform Accounts**
- Register on Connectively (formerly HARO): connectively.us — select category alerts matching client's niche
- Register on Qwoted, SourceBottle, and Featured.com
- Subscribe to HARO daily digests (Business & Finance, Technology, Lifestyle/Health, General — select relevant categories)
- Set up email filters to highlight queries from Tier 1 publications immediately

**Step 2: Create Expert Spokesperson Profile**
- Build a one-page "Expert Bio" for the client spokesperson:
  - Full name and title
  - Credentials and qualifications (degrees, certifications, years of experience)
  - Areas of expertise (5–10 specific topics)
  - Notable clients, publications, or media appearances
  - Headshot (professional)
  - Website URL and LinkedIn
- This bio is attached or referenced in every HARO pitch

**Step 3: Define Topic Filters**
- List the specific topics the client's expert can credibly address
- Create a keyword list for scanning HARO digests quickly:
  - Primary: core product/service keywords
  - Secondary: adjacent industry topics
  - Exclude: topics outside expertise (builds a "no-go" list to avoid stretch pitches)

**Step 4: Build a Response System**
- Designate who monitors queries (you, the client, or a dedicated contact)
- Set response SLA: commit to responding to qualifying queries within 2–4 hours
- Create a shared Google Doc or Notion page with:
  - Client's expert bio and quote bank
  - Pre-approved talking points by topic
  - Approved stats and data points the client can cite
  - Previously successful response examples

### Phase 2: Monitoring & Response (Daily Workflow)

**Step 5: Monitor Queries Daily**
- Check HARO digests 3x daily: morning (6–8 AM), midday (12–1 PM), afternoon (3–5 PM) — digests come out at these times
- Scan for qualifying queries using your keyword list
- Score each relevant query using the framework above
- Flag any 7+ scorers for immediate response

**Step 6: Craft the Response**
Each HARO response must have:
1. **Subject line:** Exactly as the journalist specified, PLUS a clear hook (e.g., "Re: Expert on [Topic] — [Specific angle/credential]")
2. **Opening:** 1 sentence establishing who the expert is and why they're credible for THIS specific query
3. **The answer:** The actual expert insight — specific, original, quotable (2–5 tight paragraphs or bullet points)
4. **Stats/data:** Include at least one specific stat, figure, or example to make the quote more compelling
5. **Credentials line:** "[Name] is [Title] at [Company] with [X years] experience in [specific area]"
6. **Contact info:** Name, title, company, phone, email, website URL (journalists will Google the expert)
7. **Word count:** 150–400 words ideal. Never exceed 500 words.

**Step 7: Response Quality Control**
Before sending, check:
- [ ] Does the response directly answer the journalist's specific question?
- [ ] Is the language quotable? (Reads like something a journalist would excerpt)
- [ ] Are there any claims that need citation? (Add them)
- [ ] Is the expert's name spelled correctly throughout?
- [ ] Is the website URL included correctly (this is where the link comes from)?
- [ ] Does the response avoid promotional language about the client's products/services?
- [ ] Is the response under 400 words?

**Step 8: Send and Track**
- Send via the platform's messaging system OR direct journalist email (if provided)
- Log the query in your HARO tracking sheet: platform, query topic, publication, journalist, date sent, deadline
- Do NOT follow up on the same query — journalists receive hundreds of responses and follow-ups are ignored or irritating
- Monitor for coverage: set up Google Alerts for expert's name + client brand

### Phase 3: Relationship & Scale (Ongoing)

**Step 9: Build Journalist Relationships**
- When a journalist features the client's expert, send a thank-you email immediately
- Add the journalist to a "warm journalist" list — reach out proactively for future related stories
- Follow journalists on Twitter/X; engage genuinely with their work
- When you see a journalist writing about a topic where the client is an expert, reach out proactively (reactive PR)

**Step 10: Develop a Quote Bank**
- After the first month, you'll see recurring query themes
- Pre-write polished, quotable responses for the most common topics
- This reduces response time and improves quality — templates can be customized quickly per query
- Review and update the quote bank quarterly as industry dynamics change

**Step 11: Repurpose Earned Coverage**
- When a quote is published, share on client's social media, email newsletter, and "In The Press" page
- Add the logo/outlet to the client's website ("As Seen In" section)
- Use earned media coverage as proof-of-expertise in future outreach campaigns
- High-authority coverage creates a flywheel: more press → more credibility → more HARO acceptances

## 4. Response Templates

### Template A: Standard HARO Response

**Subject:** RE: [Journalist's Subject] — [Expert Name], [Title/Credential]

Hi [Journalist First Name or "there" if unknown],

My name is [Expert Name], [Title] at [Company]. I have [X years] of experience in [specific area directly relevant to query] and [brief credential or notable context].

**[Your specific, quotable answer to their question]**

[2–3 tight paragraphs OR a bulleted list of key points. Be specific. Include data if available. Avoid filler.]

For example: [Specific example, case study, or stat that makes the quote memorable]

Happy to provide additional context, data, or a brief phone interview if helpful.

**Bio:** [Expert Name] is [Title] at [Company], where [he/she/they] [one-sentence description of expertise]. [He/She/They] has [specific credential: advised Fortune 500s, written for X publication, managed $X in assets, etc.]. More: [Website URL] | [LinkedIn URL]

[Expert Name]
[Title] | [Company]
[Email] | [Phone]
[Website]

---

### Template B: Data-Backed Response (Finance/Marketing/Tech Queries)

**Subject:** RE: [Subject] — [Expert Name], [Company] (with supporting data)

Hi [First Name / there],

[Expert Name], [Title] at [Company], here. [One-sentence credibility hook relevant to this exact query.]

**On [specific question from the query]:**

[Expert's direct, quotable answer in 2–3 sentences]

To put this in context: [specific stat or data point that supports the answer — cite source if external].

[Second insight or counterpoint that adds depth]

The key takeaway: [one bold, memorable statement that a journalist could use as a pull quote]

**About [Expert Name]:** [Title] at [Company] — [what the company does in 1 sentence], with [X years/clients/projects] in [specific area]. Website: [URL]

[Expert Name] | [Title] | [Email] | [Phone]

---

### Template C: Proactive Journalist Outreach (Reactive PR via Twitter)

**Subject:** Expert source for your piece on [Topic]

Hi [Journalist Name],

I saw your recent article on [topic they cover] — great piece. I work with [Expert Name], [Title] at [Company], who covers [related beat] extensively.

[Expert Name] has a specific perspective on [news item or trend the journalist is covering]: [1–2 sentence preview of the expert's angle or insight]

Available for a quick comment or interview if useful. Their site/bio: [URL]

[Your Name] | PR/Communications | [Contact info]

---

### Template D: Thank-You + Relationship Builder (Post-Feature)

**Subject:** Thank you for the feature — [Expert Name]

Hi [Journalist First Name],

Just wanted to say thank you for featuring [Expert Name] in your recent piece — really appreciate you including [his/her/their] perspective.

The article's been shared extensively within [our team / the [niche] community / our newsletter] — great work.

If you're ever covering [topic/beat] again and need a source on [specific area], we'd love to be a resource.

[Your Name]
On behalf of [Expert Name]

| Field | Description |
|---|---|
| Date | Query received date |
| Platform | Connectively, Qwoted, SourceBottle, etc. |
| Outlet | Publication name |
| Outlet DR | Domain Rating of publication |
| Journalist Name | If listed |
| Query Topic | Brief description |
| Query Score | 0–12 (from scoring framework) |
| Responded? | Yes / No |
| Date Responded | |
| Status | Pending / Featured / Not Used / No Response |
| Coverage URL | Article URL if published |
| Do-Follow Link? | Yes / No |
| Anchor Text | If known |
| Notes | Any context |

### Daily Workflow (30–45 minutes)
- **6:30 AM:** Scan morning HARO digest; flag and score relevant queries
- **12:30 PM:** Scan midday digest; respond to morning queries if not done
- **4:00 PM:** Final scan; follow up on any late-breaking queries; update tracking log

### Monthly Reporting
- Total queries monitored
- Total qualifying queries (score 7+)
- Responses sent
- Features/placements earned
- Placement rate (responses → features %)
- Average DR of earned placements
- Total new referring domains from HARO

## 6. Success Metrics & KPIs

### Primary KPIs
| Metric | Target |
|---|---|
| Response Rate (how often you respond to qualifying queries) | 80%+ of score 7+ queries |
| Feature Rate (responses that earn placement) | 10–25% |
| Average DR of Placements | ≥ 65 |
| % Do-Follow Links | ≥ 60% (some news sites use nofollow; even nofollow from Forbes has value) |
| Links Earned Per Month | 2–8 (quality > quantity on this tactic) |
| Time from response to publication | 1–21 days |

### Secondary KPIs
- Outlet diversity (# unique publications placing the expert)
- Journalist relationship count (warm journalists following the expert)
- Brand search volume growth
- "As Seen In" placements on client site
- Percentage of queries where response was submitted within 2 hours (speed matters)

### Benchmark: What Drives Higher Feature Rates
| Factor | Impact on Feature Rate |
|---|---|
| Response speed (< 2 hours) | +30–50% vs. slow responses |
| Specific, data-backed answer | +40% vs. generic opinions |
| Matching expert credentials to query exactly | +50% vs. stretch credentials |
| Responding to niche/specific queries vs. general | +20–30% (less competition) |
| Word count 150–300 (vs. 500+) | +20% (journalists prefer concise) |

## 7. What to Avoid

### Response Quality Mistakes
- **Generic, non-quotable responses** — "I believe [vague opinion]" is not quotable. Journalists need specific, bold, data-backed statements. Be direct.
- **Promotional language** — Never say "Our product helps with X" or "At [Company], we solve this problem." Journalists want expert opinion, not marketing copy.
- **Exceeding 500 words** — Long responses are ignored. Be concise; journalists are on deadline.
- **Not matching expertise to the query** — Responding to a query about "cryptocurrency tax law" when the expert covers "general personal finance" is a credibility stretch. Journalists notice.
- **Skipping the credentials line** — If the journalist can't verify the expert's credentials quickly, your response goes in the trash.
- **Being late** — HARO queries are time-sensitive. Responses sent after deadline are rarely considered. Establish a system to respond within 2–4 hours of receiving a qualifying query.

### Platform & Operational Mistakes
- **Monitoring only once a day** — HARO digests come out 3 times/day. Monitor all three; competitive queries fill up fast.
- **Responding to every query** — Quality over quantity. Stretching the expert's credibility for irrelevant queries burns response cycles and adds noise to your tracking.
- **Forgetting to monitor coverage** — Set up Google Alerts immediately. Many journalists don't notify sources when their quote is used.
- **No post-feature follow-through** — Failing to thank the journalist or share the coverage means missing a relationship-building opportunity.
- **One spokesperson only** — If available, develop 2–3 expert contacts at the client company who can cover different query types. This expands your qualifying query universe significantly.

### Link & SEO Expectations
- **Expecting every placement to include a do-follow link** — Many top-tier news sites (Forbes, NBC, NYT) use nofollow or no-link. The brand mention and citation value still matters for trust and referral traffic.
- **Stopping if early responses don't land** — HARO has a learning curve. Response rate improves as you refine templates, understand journalist preferences, and build a quote bank. Give it 60 days before assessing.
